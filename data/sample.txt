Retrieval-Augmented Generation, commonly known as RAG, is a technique that combines information retrieval with large language model generation. Traditional language models rely only on the knowledge stored in their parameters during training. As a result, they may provide outdated information or generate incorrect answers when asked about private documents. RAG addresses this limitation by retrieving relevant information from an external knowledge source before generating a response. A typical RAG pipeline consists of several stages. First, documents are collected and preprocessed. The text is then divided into smaller chunks to improve retrieval granularity. Each chunk is converted into a dense vector representation using an embedding model. These vectors are stored in a vector database or search index. When a user submits a query, the same embedding model converts the query into a vector. The retrieval system compares the query vector with stored vectors and returns the most relevant chunks. Embedding models are one of the most important components of a RAG system. Their purpose is to transform text into vectors that capture semantic meaning. Sentences discussing similar topics should produce vectors that are close to each other in the embedding space. This property allows semantic search to find relevant content even when the exact keywords do not match. FAISS is a popular library developed for efficient similarity search. It provides various indexing methods optimized for different scales and requirements. In small projects, developers often use IndexFlatL2 because it is simple and requires no training. For larger datasets, more advanced indexing techniques can significantly reduce retrieval latency while maintaining acceptable accuracy. The quality of retrieval often determines the overall performance of a RAG system. Even the most advanced language model cannot provide accurate answers if the retrieval stage returns irrelevant information. For this reason, practitioners frequently experiment with chunk size, overlap settings, embedding models, and retrieval strategies. Improvements in retrieval quality often lead to larger gains than simply replacing the language model with a more powerful one.

A vector is a mathematical representation of direction and magnitude.
Embeddings convert text into vectors.
SentenceTransformer is commonly used for generating sentence embeddings.
Semantic similarity can be measured using cosine similarity.
Cosine similarity focuses on vector direction rather than magnitude.
Vector normalization converts vectors to unit length.
After normalization, inner product becomes equivalent to cosine similarity.
FAISS supports both L2 distance and inner product search.
Top-K retrieval returns the K most relevant chunks.
Chunking improves retrieval granularity.
